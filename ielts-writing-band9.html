📈 Mẫu 1: Line Graph — Band 9

ĐỀ BÀI
The graph below shows the proportion of the population aged 65 and over between 1940 and 2040 in three different countries.
BÀI MẪU BAND 9

The line graph illustrates the percentage of elderly people (aged 65+) in Japan, Sweden, and the USA over a 100-year period from 1940 to 2040.

Overall, it is evident that the proportion of senior citizens has increased and is projected to continue rising in all three nations, with Japan expected to experience the most dramatic surge.

In 1940, the USA had the highest percentage of elderly residents at approximately 9%, while Sweden and Japan stood at around 7% and 5% respectively. Over the following decades, Sweden overtook the USA, reaching roughly 15% by 1990, compared to about 13% for the USA. Japan, by contrast, remained relatively stable at around 5% until 1980.

Looking ahead, Japan is forecast to witness a remarkable increase, soaring to approximately 27% by 2040 — surpassing both Sweden (25%) and the USA (23%). This represents a fivefold increase from its 1940 figure.

📊 Mẫu 2: Bar Chart — Band 8.5

ĐỀ BÀI
The chart below shows the number of men and women in further education in Britain in three periods.
BÀI MẪU BAND 8.5

The bar chart compares the number of males and females enrolled in further education in Britain across three distinct periods: 1970/71, 1980/81, and 1990/91.

Overall, female participation grew substantially over the two decades, eventually exceeding that of males, while male enrolment showed a more modest increase.

In 1970/71, approximately 1,000 thousand men were in further education, compared to only around 700 thousand women. A decade later, male numbers rose marginally to about 1,100 thousand, whereas female numbers climbed more sharply to roughly 950 thousand, narrowing the gap considerably.

By 1990/91, the trend had reversed entirely: women outnumbered men with approximately 1,550 thousand versus 1,200 thousand. This represents a doubling of female participation since 1970.

⚙️ Mẫu 3: Process — Band 8.5

ĐỀ BÀI
The diagram below shows how chocolate is produced.
BÀI MẪU BAND 8.5

The diagram illustrates the process by which chocolate is manufactured, from the initial harvesting of cacao beans to the final production of liquid chocolate.

Overall, it is clear that chocolate production is a lengthy process, involving ten distinct stages that can be broadly divided into outdoor cultivation and indoor manufacturing.

At the outset, ripe cacao pods are harvested from trees and the beans are subsequently extracted. These beans are then spread out and left to dry in the sun for approximately two weeks, after which they are packed into sacks and transported to the factory by either train or lorry.

Upon arrival at the factory, the beans are roasted at a temperature of 350°C before being crushed into smaller fragments. The resulting cacao nibs are then ground into a paste, which is ultimately pressed to produce liquid chocolate.

✅ Mẫu 4: Agree/Disagree — Band 9

ĐỀ BÀI
Some people believe that universities should focus on providing academic skills rather than practical training for future careers. To what extent do you agree or disagree?
BÀI MẪU BAND 9

It is often argued that higher education institutions should prioritize academic knowledge over vocational preparation. I firmly disagree with this viewpoint, as I believe universities should strike a balance between theoretical understanding and practical competence.

Admittedly, academic skills form the intellectual foundation upon which professional expertise is built. Critical thinking, research methodology, and analytical reasoning are indispensable tools that enable graduates to navigate complex problems throughout their careers. Furthermore, a strong theoretical grounding fosters innovation, as many groundbreaking discoveries have emerged from purely academic pursuits.

Nevertheless, the modern job market demands a workforce equipped with hands-on skills and industry-relevant experience. Employers increasingly report a skills gap between what graduates know and what the workplace requires. Universities that incorporate internships, project-based learning, and industry partnerships produce more employable graduates who can contribute meaningfully from day one.

In conclusion, while academic rigour remains the cornerstone of university education, institutions must evolve to meet the demands of a rapidly changing economy. A well-rounded curriculum that integrates both elements will best serve students' long-term interests.

💬 Mẫu 5: Discuss Both Views — Band 8.5

ĐỀ BÀI
Some people think that the best way to reduce crime is to give longer prison sentences. Others believe there are better alternative ways of reducing crime. Discuss both views and give your opinion.
BÀI MẪU BAND 8.5

The question of how to most effectively combat crime remains a contentious issue. While some advocate for harsher punitive measures, others contend that preventative approaches yield superior results. This essay will examine both perspectives before presenting my own view.

Proponents of longer sentences argue that severe penalties serve as a powerful deterrent. The logic is straightforward: potential offenders are less likely to commit crimes if they face substantial prison terms. Moreover, incarceration removes dangerous individuals from society, thereby protecting the public during the sentence period.

On the other hand, critics point to evidence suggesting that recidivism rates remain stubbornly high regardless of sentence length. They advocate for investment in education, rehabilitation programmes, and addressing the root causes of criminal behaviour, such as poverty, substance abuse, and lack of opportunity. Countries like Norway, which prioritize rehabilitation, boast significantly lower reoffending rates than nations with punitive systems.

In my view, a multifaceted approach combining proportionate sentencing with robust rehabilitation programmes represents the most effective strategy for reducing crime sustainably.

🔧 Mẫu 6: Problem/Solution — Band 8.5

ĐỀ BÀI
In many cities, the increasing number of cars is causing serious problems. What are the problems? What solutions can you suggest?
BÀI MẪU BAND 8.5

The proliferation of private vehicles in urban areas has given rise to a number of pressing concerns. This essay will outline the key problems and propose viable solutions.

The most significant issue is traffic congestion, which leads to longer commute times, increased stress levels, and reduced productivity. Equally concerning is the environmental impact: vehicle emissions contribute substantially to air pollution and climate change, with transportation accounting for approximately 27% of global greenhouse gas emissions.

To address these challenges, governments should invest heavily in public transport infrastructure. Cities like Tokyo and Singapore demonstrate that efficient, affordable public transit can dramatically reduce car dependency. Additionally, implementing congestion charges — as London has done — can discourage unnecessary car use. Finally, promoting remote work and developing cycling infrastructure offer cost-effective complementary measures.

In conclusion, while the challenges posed by increasing car numbers are formidable, a combination of infrastructure investment, financial incentives, and lifestyle changes can significantly mitigate the impact.
