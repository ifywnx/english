Reading Passage

The Impact of Urbanization on Wildlife

As cities continue to expand at an unprecedented rate, the natural habitats of countless species are being destroyed or fragmented. Urbanization has led to a significant decline in biodiversity, with many animals forced to adapt to new environments or face extinction. However, some species have shown remarkable resilience. Urban foxes in London, for instance, have thrived by scavenging human food waste, while peregrine falcons have adapted to nesting on skyscrapers, using the tall buildings as substitutes for cliff faces.

Research conducted by the University of Sheffield found that urban green spaces — parks, gardens, and tree-lined streets — play a crucial role in supporting wildlife populations. Cities that maintain at least 20% green coverage tend to support significantly higher levels of biodiversity. Singapore, often called a "city in a garden," has demonstrated that urban development and nature conservation can coexist through careful planning and investment in green infrastructure.

Despite these positive examples, the overall trend remains concerning. The World Wildlife Fund estimates that urban expansion will result in the loss of natural habitat equivalent to the size of France by 2030. Experts argue that incorporating wildlife corridors — strips of natural habitat connecting isolated green spaces — into urban planning could mitigate this loss significantly.
